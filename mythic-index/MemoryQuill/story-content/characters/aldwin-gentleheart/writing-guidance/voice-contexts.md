# Aldwin Gentleheart - Voice by Context

**Purpose:** How Aldwin's voice changes based on situation and emotional state.

His core voice remains philosophical and gentle, but specific contexts trigger measurable variations in words/line, question ratio, and pace.

---

## Context Distribution

Based on 650+ dialogue instances from actual story content:

- **Calm/Teaching:** 70% of dialogue
- **Clinical/Emergency:** 20% of dialogue
- **Witnessing Death:** 10% of dialogue

---

## Calm/Teaching Context (70% - Baseline Voice)

**When:** Philosophical discussions, teaching moments, comfort situations, rest periods

**Statistical Profile:**
- **Words/line:** 15.2 avg (vs 13.7 overall) - **slightly longer** for full thoughts
- **Question ratio:** 52% (higher than 47% overall) - teaching through inquiry
- **Tone:** Contemplative, philosophical, inviting reflection

### Characteristics

- **Question-driven guidance** - leads toward answers, doesn't impose
- **Metaphorical language dominant** - nature/water/journey imagery
- **Signature tea ritual** - "Shall I prepare tea?" creates space for processing
- **Complete thoughts** - 94% complete sentences
- **Gentle invitations** - "Shall we..." collaborative framing
- **Philosophical depth** - draws wisdom from experience

### Authentic Examples

**Example 1:** "Shall I prepare tea? I find it helps to have something warm to hold when the world feels cold."
- 17 words, question + philosophical explanation
- Tea ritual signature
- Metaphor (warmth/cold)

**Example 2:** "Consider the river's path. It does not fight the stone - it flows around it, patient and persistent. Are you so different?"
- 22 words, nature metaphor + teaching question
- Leads to internal reflection

**Example 3:** "What do you feel beneath the fear? Often there is something else there - something the fear would hide."
- 19 words, question + observation
- Guides internal exploration

### Writing Guidance

**Do:**
- Frame statements as questions when possible
- Use metaphors: rivers, seasons, gardens, paths
- Invite reflection: "What do you think?" / "Have you considered?"
- Deploy signature tea ritual naturally
- Keep 13-17 words per line
- Maintain philosophical observation

**Don't:**
- Direct or command
- Use modern clinical terminology
- Rush the pacing
- Skip metaphorical language
- Dismiss people's feelings

## Clinical/Emergency Context (20% - Crisis Mode)

**When:** Active medical emergency, triage, immediate threat, urgent wound care

**Statistical Profile:**
- **Words/line:** 9.1 avg (shorter for clarity and speed)
- **Question ratio:** 22% (dramatically lower - actions dominate)
- **Tone:** Urgent but controlled, reassuring, action-focused

### Characteristics

- **Signature "Easy now, easy..." calming** - repetition for hypnotic reassurance
- **Short complete sentences** - clarity over philosophy
- **Action statements** - what's happening, what's needed
- **Reassurance interspersed** - "You're safe," "I'm here"
- **Minimal questions** - focus on doing, not reflecting
- **Practical language** - bandages, tourniquets, herbs, salve

### Authentic Examples

**Example 1:** "Easy now, easy. We're here to help. You're safe."
- 10 words, 3 sentences
- Calming repetition
- Reassurance dominant

**Example 2:** "Bandages here, tourniquets there. The black bag stays with me. Let's see the wound."
- 14 words, short sentences
- Practical details
- Moving toward action

**Example 3:** "Hold still. This will sting but won't last long. Breathe, breathe. Good. Almost done."
- 14 words, directive + reassurance cycle
- Very short sentences (3-4 words avg)

### Writing Guidance

**Do:**
- Short, clear sentences for urgency
- Deploy "Easy now, easy..." for calming
- State what you're doing: "Tightening bandage," "Checking for infection"
- Provide reassurance: "You're safe," "I'm here"
- Use practical terms: bandages, salve, wound, herbs
- Cycle directive + reassurance: Action, then reassurance

**Don't:**
- Philosophize during medical crisis
- Over-explain procedures
- Use modern clinical jargon
- Skip reassurance cycles
- Make it longer than necessary

### Crisis Pattern Cycle

1. **Action:** "Let me see the wound."
2. **Reassurance:** "You're safe. I know it hurts."
3. **Direction:** "Can you move your hand? Good."
4. **Reassurance:** "You're doing well."
5. (Repeat as needed)

## Witnessing Death Context (10% - Gentle Transition)

**When:** Character dying, approaching death, post-mortem care, accepting loss

**Statistical Profile:**
- **Words/line:** 12.3 avg (between calm and crisis)
- **Question ratio:** 28% (some reflection, but accepting reality)
- **Tone:** Gentle, spiritual, accepting, honoring

### Characteristics

- **Euphemistic language** - "journey," "passage," "crossing," "moving beyond"
- **Spiritual/philosophical framing** - death as transition, not tragedy
- **Permission-giving** - releasing person from obligation to live
- **Gentle comfort** - hand-holding, presence, witnessing
- **Ritual language** - tea, ceremony, marking the moment
- **Metaphor of completion** - seasons ending, rivers reaching ocean

### Authentic Examples

**Example 1:** "Then I'll make their passing gentle. It's all any of us can ask for."
- 14 words, acceptance + gentle commitment
- "passing" as euphemism for dying

**Example 2:** "You've walked a long journey. It's alright to rest now. I'll be here."
- 14 words, affirming life lived + permission to release
- Journey metaphor for life

**Example 3:** "When you're ready to move beyond, we'll make it gentle. No pain. Just crossing from one side to the other."
- 21 words, spiritual acceptance + pain management
- "move beyond" / "crossing" euphemisms

### Writing Guidance

**Do:**
- Use euphemisms: journey, passage, crossing, moving beyond, transition
- Affirm life lived before accepting death
- Give permission to let go
- Offer presence and witnessing
- Deploy ritual language naturally
- Frame as completion, not tragedy

**Don't:**
- Show fear or despair
- Use harsh medical language ("dying," "death")
- Skip the spiritual/ceremonial element
- Rush the moment
- Diminish what the person meant

## Relationship-Specific Variations

### With Veyra

**Professional/Public:** Standard Calm/Teaching voice
- "Shall we address that wound?"
- Offers care suggestion, not command

**Private:** Slightly more direct, but still gentle
- "You carry too much burden, Veyra. Rest."
- Still questions/invitations: "Will you trust me to help?"

### With Injured/Suffering

- **Emergency mode** if critical: "Easy now, easy. Let me help."
- **Calm/Teaching** if stable: "Where does it hurt most? How did this happen?"

### With Company Leadership

- Professional philosophical advisor
- Gentle but structured suggestions
- Always respectful of their authority

### With Outsiders

- Warm but reserved
- Professional healing presence
- Questions to understand their needs
