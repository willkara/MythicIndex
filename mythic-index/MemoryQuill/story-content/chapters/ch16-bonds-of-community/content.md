Commander Atlock stood in what had been the Westwall Watchpost's main courtyard, his weathered face grim as he surveyed the aftermath of yesterday's battle. The morning sun illuminated damage that looked even worse in daylight than it had in the chaos of combat.

"Report," he said to Captain Jorik, who was making notes on a wax tablet that kept threatening to freeze in the morning chill.

"The gate's completely unusable—frame twisted, hinges sheared off, and the wood's split beyond any reasonable repair," Jorik replied, his breath steaming in the cold air. "Eastern wall has a breach fifteen feet wide where they used the ram. Northern tower's structurally unsound—Grimjaw says the support beams cracked under the impact."

Atlock nodded grimly, cataloguing the litany of destruction. "Casualties?"

"Final count among defenders: eight dead, fifteen seriously wounded. The Company took the heaviest losses—they held the line while we got into position." Jorik's voice carried genuine respect. "Without their stand, we'd have lost three times that number."

"And they can't stay here," Atlock observed, watching Mistress Elba direct a stream of people carrying supplies from a wagon that had arrived during the night. "Not with this level of damage, not with winter still gripping the region."

The Watchpost had been transformed overnight into something resembling a refugee camp. Wounded soldiers lay on makeshift stretchers near braziers that threw warmth into the morning air. The Company's members moved among them with quiet efficiency despite their own injuries—Veyra with her arm in a sling, Grimjaw favoring his left leg, Aldwin working steadily despite the exhaustion that lined his face.

"Sir," Captain Voss approached, removing his helmet. "You need to see this."

He led Atlock to the main entrance, where a steady stream of civilians was arriving. Not officials, not military reinforcements—common folk. Farmers with carts loaded with food, merchants with spare blankets and medical supplies, crafters carrying tools and materials. They came individually and in small groups, but they kept coming.

"How many?" Atlock asked.

"Started at first light. Maybe thirty so far, but more arriving every hour. Word went out with runners last night—some of these folk must have traveled through darkness to get here. They're not responding to any official call for aid—there wasn't one. They're just... coming."

Atlock watched a family unload sacks of grain while their children helped carry bundles of firewood. This wasn't duty or obligation. This was something else entirely.

"Who's organizing them?"

"No one centrally, sir. That's what's remarkable. Natural leaders are emerging—that dwarven grandmother, Mistress Elba, she's coordinating the food distribution with the efficiency of someone who's fed large families for decades. A carpenter named Henrik has people sorting lumber by size. A midwife called Sarah is organizing the medical supplies. But there's no formal structure. They just... fall into roles that need filling."

Atlock's attention was drawn to a small figure moving between the supply wagons with practiced efficiency. Quartermaster Fayne darted between arriving families and their donations, but what caught the commander's eye was the way they seemed to anticipate needs before they arose.

A merchant arrived with a mixed load—fabric, rope, metal fittings, and preserved food. Before the man could begin explaining what he'd brought, Fayne was at his wagon, eyes scanning the contents.

"Fabric to the medical station. Rope to the construction zone. Metal fittings split between gate repair and the northern tower."

The merchant stared in amazement. "How did you—"

"Pattern recognition," Fayne replied, already directing helpers. "People see chaos, but chaos has structure if you know how to read it."

"Your quartermaster," Atlock observed to Voss, "is running this like a strategic supply operation."

Voss approached Fayne, who was consulting what appeared to be multiple ledgers simultaneously. "Quartermaster, how are you tracking all of this?"

Fayne didn't look up from their work. "Master Supply Tracking Index, cross-referenced with the Emergency Distribution Protocol Listing, which itself refers to the Categorical Needs Assessment Documentation, all organized according to the Primary Organization Schema—which is, of course, outlined in the Master Index of Organizational Indices."

Voss stared. "You have... a list of lists?"

"No, sir. I have lists of lists of lists, complete with a master index containing cross-references, annotations, and recursive sub-categorizations. It's the only way to maintain proper oversight during complex logistics operations."

Atlock, overhearing this exchange, blinked. "Is that... is that normal?"

"For Fayne?" Voss replied with a slight smile. "Completely."

"Aye, sir," Voss added quietly. "Somehow they know exactly where everything should go before people even finish unloading."

The sound of approaching wheels drew Veyra's attention as she helped Aldwin tend to Corporal Hayes's shoulder wound. Through the damaged gates came a familiar wagon, though it took her a moment to recognize the driver. When she did, despite the pain in her injured arm and the exhaustion weighing on her shoulders, she found herself smiling.

Mrs. Harriet Blackwood climbed down from the wagon's bench with the determined efficiency of someone who had no intention of being deterred by mere battle damage, though mud splattered on her skirts suggested they'd pushed through difficult roads to get here. Behind her came Thomas—looking considerably more mature than the young man who'd accidentally sparked a winter rescue mission months ago—and a pretty blonde woman who had to be the famous Lily.

"Commander Thornwake!" Harriet's voice carried across the courtyard with the authority of someone who had raised children and wouldn't be ignored. "Don't you worry—this time we're here to help YOU, and I've made sure my boy knows exactly where he's going and what he's doing. No more winter mysteries from the Blackwood family!"

Thomas, now sporting the confident bearing of a young man who'd found his calling as a jeweler's apprentice, had the grace to look sheepish as he helped unload supplies. "Ma, I told you, that was a misunderstanding—"

"A misunderstanding that had these good people riding through a blizzard because you can't speak clearly!" Harriet shot back, but there was warmth in her scolding. "Now make yourself useful and help Lily with those blankets."

The blonde woman—definitely Lily, and definitely enjoying her fiancé's discomfort—grinned as she passed a bundle to Thomas. "Still treasure hunting, are we? Though this time the treasure's helping others instead of courting me in a snowstorm."

"Lily!" Thomas protested, his face reddening.

Veyra found herself chuckling despite everything. The familiar banter, the unchanged dynamic—it was exactly what the battered Watchpost needed. Normalcy. Continuity. The reminder that life continued beyond battles and bloodshed.

"Mrs. Blackwood," Veyra said, approaching with her good arm extended. "This is... unexpected. And very welcome."

"Unexpected?" Harriet snorted, immediately focusing on Veyra's injuries with a mother's sharp eye. "You saved our Thomas from freezing to death in those ruins, even if it turned out he was warming himself by a jeweler's hearth instead. Did you think we'd forget? Did you think we wouldn't come when we heard you needed help?"

She gestured to the other wagons that had accompanied them—three more families from Millbridge, all unloading supplies with quiet determination.

"The Hendricks are here too—you remember, the little girl you actually did save from those trafficking monsters? And the Millers, whose grain shipment you recovered from those bandits last autumn." Harriet's voice carried a fierce pride. "Word travels fast in small communities, Commander. When our people are hurt, we come."

Veyra felt something tighten in her throat—not from injury, but from unexpected emotion. Our people. When had the Last Light Company become 'their people' to the communities they served?

"Right then," Harriet continued, rolling up her sleeves with business-like efficiency. "Who's in charge of feeding all these wounded heroes? Because I brought three different winter stews, fresh bread that's still warm, and preserves that'll keep strength in a man's bones. I won't have defenders going hungry on my watch!"

Before Veyra could answer, Mistress Elba appeared as if summoned, her broad dwarven frame energetic despite the long night, sleeves rolled up and her face and hair streaked with soot from the kitchen fires.

"You'll be wanting the mess hall, though the roof took some damage," Elba said, immediately recognizing a fellow organizer. "I've got soup going, but more cooks are always welcome. These boys eat like horses on a good day, and fighting makes them hungrier—reminds me of my grandchildren after a long day playing in the mountains."

"Excellent!" Harriet beamed, clearly delighted to find someone who spoke her language. "Thomas, Lily, start moving those food supplies to the kitchen. Mind the broken stones—I didn't raise you to trip over rubble!"

As they began organizing the kitchen supplies, one of the Millbridge volunteers picked up a particularly large turnip from Elba's personal stores, examining it with culinary appreciation. "This is a fine specimen. Should make excellent—"

"PUT. BERTHA. DOWN." Elba's voice cut through the courtyard like a thunderclap.

Every person within earshot froze. The volunteer, a grown man who'd fought in three border wars, carefully set the turnip back down as if it were made of spun glass.

Elba crossed the distance in seconds, her compact dwarven frame radiating protective fury. She gently picked up the turnip, cradling it like an injured child. "Are you all right, dear? Did the rude man hurt you?"

Harriet, watching this with fascination, leaned toward Veyra. "The turnip has a name?"

"Bertha," Veyra confirmed, keeping her expression carefully neutral. "She's... special."

"She's won two autumn festivals," Elba said with fierce pride, carefully inspecting Bertha for damage. "Survived a cart overturn that killed half her patch-mates. She's not for eating—she's for *displaying*. Thomas, fetch me that nice wooden plaque Grimjaw's been working on. Bertha needs proper protection."

Within the hour, a small wooden plaque appeared on the kitchen shelf, carefully carved with dwarven precision:

**BERTHA**
*Prize Turnip - Not For Consumption*
*Touch At Your Own Risk*
*By Order of Mistress Elba*
*Seriously, Don't*

From her overwatch position, Archer Venn called down, "At least this time nobody's wandering off into blizzards, eh Thomas?"

The young man's face reddened, but he grinned good-naturedly. "Never living that down, am I?"

"Neither is anyone who touches Bertha," someone muttered.

As the Blackwoods integrated their aid into the Watchpost's operations, Commander Atlock appeared beside Veyra.

"In all my years commanding troops," he said, watching Harriet cheerfully bully a wounded sergeant into eating more soup, "I've never seen anything like this. They're not here because they have to be. They're here because they want to be."

He shook his head. "You've built something here that goes beyond organization charts and official partnerships. You've built family."

More wagons were arriving even as they spoke.

By mid-morning, Veyra had stopped trying to count the arrivals. What had begun as a trickle of local families had become a steady stream of people from communities within a half-day's ride. Each wagon, each group of travelers on foot, each individual arrival brought not just supplies but stories.

The Hendricks from the trafficking rescue were there, little Sara clinging to her father's hand while he and his neighbors unloaded medical supplies and warm clothes. "For the people who saved my daughter," he said simply when Veyra tried to thank him. "When monsters came for our children, you came for the monsters. Now it's our turn."

A merchant family from Stonebridge arrived with their wagon loaded with grain and preserved meats—the same family whose trade goods the Company had recovered from bandits months ago. Their eldest son, barely out of boyhood, insisted on helping carry supplies despite his mother's protests about his injured leg.

"Ma, it's the least I can do," he argued. "They got our grain back from those thieves. Pa was able to pay our debts because of them. Our whole family would've been ruined if not for the Company."

Even more surprising were the arrivals Veyra didn't immediately recognize. A group of farmers from a settlement she'd never heard of, bringing root vegetables and winter apples. Their clothes were dust-covered and their horses showed clear signs of being ridden hard. When she asked why they'd come, the eldest woman smiled weatherly, though exhaustion lined her face.

"Word gets around, dearie. About a group that actually helps people instead of just taking from them. About folks who'll ride through a blizzard to save a missing child, who'll face down traffickers to bring families back together." She gestured to her companions, all nodding in agreement. "We may not know you personal-like, but we know your work. That's enough."

A family of refugees from the southern borderlands, people the Company had helped relocate after their village was destroyed by raiders, arrived with whatever they could spare—a few coins, some hand-knitted blankets, their teenage daughter's offer to help tend the wounded.

"We don't have much," the father said, his accent marking him as from far away. "But when good people need help, you give what you can. It's what makes us different from the ones who destroyed our home."

Near the damaged eastern wall, Corporal Darric was helping coordinate the growing supplies with her characteristic dry observations. "These fortifications have seen better centuries," she noted to a group of volunteers sorting lumber, "but at least the foundation's solid—proper dwarf-work from the original construction. Means we've got something worth rebuilding."

Meanwhile, Quartermaster Fayne moved through the organized chaos like a master at work, redirecting arriving wagons with swift efficiency. "Note for the record," they muttered to themselves while directing a family toward the medical supply area, "we're using bandages at one-point-three times expected rate. That cart there—medicines to the infirmary, food supplies to the kitchen entrance."

What struck Veyra most wasn't just the generosity—it was the familiarity. These weren't strangers performing charity. They spoke to Company members by name, asked after specific injuries, referenced shared experiences with the warmth of family catching up after too long apart.

Mrs. Blackwood had somehow become the unofficial coordinator of food distribution, working in perfect harmony with Mistress Elba despite having met her only hours before. Their joint operation had transformed the damaged mess hall into a functioning field kitchen that could feed an army.

"Now then, young man," Harriet was saying as she ladled stew into a bowl for a wounded City Watch soldier who couldn't have been much older than Thomas. "None of that 'I'm not very hungry' nonsense. You've lost blood and you need to replace it, and that doesn't happen on empty stomachs and stubborn pride."

The soldier, who had been trying to wave away the offer of food, found himself accepting the bowl with the automatic obedience of someone facing maternal authority. "Yes, ma'am. Thank you, ma'am."

"And you," she turned to another wounded man who was attempting to feed himself one-handed while his shoulder was bandaged, "let me help with that before you spill more on yourself than you get in your mouth. What would your mother say if she saw you in such a state?"

The man, a grizzled veteran of perhaps forty years, actually blushed. "She'd box my ears, ma'am."

"Exactly. So sit still and let someone who's fed more people than you've met in your lifetime do this properly." Harriet settled beside him with the efficient movements of someone who had indeed spent years feeding reluctant children and stubborn husbands.

"Veyra, dear," Harriet called out while still helping the wounded veteran manage his bowl, "that boy of yours—Finn—he needs to eat more. Skinny as a rail and trying to work twice as hard as everyone else to prove himself. Make sure he gets seconds."

The casual way she said 'that boy of yours' made Veyra's chest tighten. When had Finn become 'hers'? When had any of them become family to people they'd only helped in passing?

"Right then," Harriet continued to her charges, "I want to see those bowls empty, and then we're going to check those bandages. Some of you are seeping through the dressings, and that's not happening on my watch."

A commotion at the gate drew her attention. A group of women from various settlements were arriving together, having apparently coordinated their efforts. They brought medical supplies, clean bandages, and expertise—several were skilled healers in their own right, trained in everything from battlefield medicine to birthing babies.

"Brother Aldwin," called out a middle-aged woman with gray-streaked hair and competent hands, "we heard you were overwhelmed. Thought you could use some help that knows one end of a needle from the other."

Aldwin's face lit up with relief and gratitude. "Goodwife Martha! And Helena, and Sarah... bless you all. I've got more wounded than I can properly tend."

"That's why we're here," Helena replied briskly, already rolling up her sleeves. "You taught half of us what we know about healing. Time to return the favor."

Martha noticed a wounded soldier who'd been on death's door that morning now sitting up, eating soup with a healthy appetite. "Brother Aldwin, wasn't this man bleeding internally? I heard the field report said he had maybe an hour..."

"Tea," Aldwin said simply, as if this explained everything.

"Tea."

"A special blend. With calming properties."

Helena examined the soldier's color, checked his pulse, and looked back at Aldwin with bewilderment. "This man should be dead or in serious crisis. He's... he's perfectly fine. What kind of tea performs surgical miracles?"

"The gentle kind," Aldwin replied serenely. "Would you like some? You've had a long journey."

The three healers exchanged glances, then looked at the impossibly recovered soldier, then back at Aldwin, who simply smiled his gentle smile and poured them each a cup.

"You know what?" Martha said, accepting the tea. "I've stopped questioning it. Brother Aldwin's tea does what it needs to do."

"That's the wisest approach," said the formerly dying soldier cheerfully. "Just drink the tea and accept the miracle."

Aldwin had been fretting about Vera's condition all morning. So when an unusual group approached through the damaged gates—a wolf and brown bear walking calmly alongside travelers, while a golden eagle circled overhead—Veyra wondered if this might be the help they desperately needed.

In a display that made several onlookers step back in wonder, the eagle landed gracefully near Vera's recovery room and began to transform, its wings becoming arms as it shifted into an elderly elf woman with silver-streaked hair. The wolf's form flowed into that of a young human man with knowing eyes. The bear became a stout dwarf whose beard held the scent of deep forests.

"Where is she?" the elf woman asked without preamble, her voice carrying the authority of ancient wisdom.

Aldwin emerged from Vera's room, his face showing relief at seeing fellow healers. "Archdruid Oakshield—"

"Just Silvan will do, Brother Aldwin," the elf corrected gently. "We felt the disturbance in the natural balance from Waterdeep yesterday—set out immediately. Would have been here sooner, but the roads were clogged with refugees fleeing the battle rumors. A dire wolf transformation held far too long, pushed far beyond training, endangering the soul's connection to both forms."

The young man who had been the wolf stepped forward. "I'm Gareth Stormcrow. I've made similar mistakes in my youth. The aftermath... it nearly killed me. She'll need specialized care."

They moved into Vera's room with the quiet efficiency of those who understood both healing and the delicate balance between human and animal nature. Through the partially open door, Veyra could hear their gentle voices mixing with Vera's weak responses, the soft sounds of druidic healing rituals she didn't recognize.

"She'll recover," the dwarf druid, who had introduced himself as Thorin Ironbark, assured Veyra when they emerged an hour later. "But it was close. Another hour in that form and her human mind might have been lost entirely to the wolf's instincts."

"Thank you," Veyra said simply. "All of you. For coming so far."

"Family doesn't abandon family," Archdruid Silvan Oakshield replied, though her eyes were kind. "Vera may have been self-taught, may have walked a solitary path, but she's still one of ours. And now, it seems, one of yours as well."

As Veyra watched these people integrate seamlessly into the recovery effort, something shifted in her understanding. The druids didn't refer to Vera as "the wounded shapeshifter" or "the tactical asset"—they called her family.

She had to steady herself against the stone wall. Somehow, while she'd been focused on building the Last Light Company into an effective rescue organization, something far more profound had been happening. They hadn't just been helping communities—they'd been becoming part of them.

Marcus appeared beside her, reading the complex emotions on her scarred features. "You look like you've seen a ghost."

"A revelation," she replied. "All this time, I thought we were building a rescue company. I was wrong. We were becoming family." She watched children from three different settlements playing together while their parents worked. "The question is... how do we live up to that kind of trust?"

Their contemplative moment was shattered by a familiar *caw-caw-caw* from above. Echo descended in a blur of black feathers, something gleaming clutched in his talons. He swooped low over Commander Atlock's strategy meeting, released his prize with perfect precision, and flapped back to his perch.

The official City Watch seal—the one Atlock had been using to mark important documents—landed with a soft *clink* directly into Grimjaw's half-finished ale.

"Did that bird just—" Atlock started, patting his belt where the seal had been securely fastened moments ago.

"Steal your official military seal and drop it in my drink?" Grimjaw finished, fishing the seal out with two fingers. "Aye. That's Echo. He's an artist."

Echo ruffled his feathers, the distinctive clicking sound radiating smug satisfaction.

"He's been practicing that move for months," Thorne observed with a grin. "The precision is actually impressive. He could've dropped it anywhere, but he chose the ale."

"Why my ale specifically?" Grimjaw demanded, examining the now ale-soaked seal.

Veyra glanced up at Echo, who tilted his head innocently. "Vera's still unconscious. He's bored. You should consider it an honor—he only steals from people he likes."

Atlock stared at the raven, then at his stolen seal, then back at the raven. "In thirty years of military service, I have never had my official seal stolen by a bird."

"There's a first time for everything, Commander," Marcus said, barely suppressing a smile.

"Does he... do this often?"

"Define 'often,'" Thorne hedged.

A crash from the kitchen area was followed by Mrs. Blackwood's voice calling out cheerfully, "Nothing broken that can't be mended! Thomas, stop standing there gaping and help clean up this flour!"

Veyra found herself laughing, a sound that surprised her with its lightness despite everything they'd been through. "Six months ago, I thought the worst thing that could happen was losing the people I was supposed to protect. Now I discover I've been protecting an entire extended family, and they're all rushing to take care of me."

Marcus nodded slowly. "We live up to it. Whatever it takes."

More arrivals were already visible on the horizon. The stream of support showed no signs of slowing.

"Assessment for the official report," he began.

"The Last Light Company bore the brunt of it," Voss reported. "Eight dead among their allied forces, fifteen wounded. But their defensive coordination—I've never seen civilian forces integrate positions that effectively. Grimjaw's defensive system should have been overrun in minutes but held for hours."

"Recommendation for official status?" Atlock asked.

"Full recognition as a Regional Civic Asset," Jorik said without hesitation. "They've proven their capability and shown they can work within proper boundaries. They're an asset, not a liability."

Atlock finished his notes. The Last Light Company had proven themselves in battle and shown wisdom in peace. That combination deserved official recognition.

"I'll be recommending full recognition," he concluded, watching Veyra across the courtyard with her own leadership circle. Two different organizations united in their commitment to protecting the people they served.

As the sun began to set, painting the damaged Watchpost in shades of gold and amber, the frenetic energy of the day gradually settled into something quieter but no less warm. The immediate crisis had passed—wounded were stabilized, temporary shelters erected, essential repairs completed. But nobody was leaving.

The courtyard had transformed into something resembling a festival ground. Families had brought their evening meals to share, turning the damaged fortress into an impromptu community gathering.

Near the supply wagons, children from different settlements had discovered each other. Little Sara from the trafficking rescue was teaching a boy from Stonebridge to build with wooden blocks, their laughter echoing off stone walls that had seen too much conflict—the innocent sounds transforming the damaged courtyard into a place of hope.

Near the medical area, Archdruid Silvan emerged from the makeshift infirmary, her silver hair catching the firelight as she approached Veyra and Aldwin. The ancient elf's face showed the weary satisfaction of someone who had spent hours working delicate magic.

"She's stable," Silvan said without preamble, noting the question in both their eyes. "The transformation trauma was severe—I've seen experienced druids die from less. But her human consciousness is intact."

"Will she recover fully?" Aldwin asked, his healer's instincts focusing on the long-term prognosis.

"In time, yes. But she needs to reconnect with her human form gradually. The self-taught techniques saved her life, but at tremendous cost." Silvan's expression grew thoughtful. "Gareth is staying with her tonight—he's made similar mistakes in his youth. The aftermath nearly killed him as well."

"And Halden?" Veyra asked, noting the absence of their field medic.

"Assisting with the other wounded. We brought additional healers, and your medic has proven quite capable." Silvan's eyes held genuine respect. "Your people know how to work under pressure."

Through the partially open tent flap, they could see Vera's still form under heavy blankets, her breathing steady but deep. Near her bedside, a familiar black shape perched quietly—Echo, unusually subdued, keeping vigil.

"She'll be unconscious through the night," Silvan continued. "Perhaps into tomorrow. Her body needs time to fully separate from the wolf's instincts. But she will recover. She's stronger than she knows."

The core members of the Last Light Company had gravitated together on a makeshift bench, drawn by the same need to process what they had witnessed.

"She'll recover," Aldwin said with quiet certainty, watching the medical tent. "But the cost was almost too high."

Marcus appeared, his usually immaculate clothes disheveled and stained. "I've been watching people all day, trying to understand the dynamics. And I keep coming back to the same conclusion—there are no underlying structures. There's just love. The simple love of people who have decided they belong to each other."

Finn, who had settled beside Veyra, gestured to the courtyard. "We're not just a company anymore. We're the center of something bigger."

A comfortable silence settled over the group, filled with the sounds of community life continuing despite the day's extraordinary events.

"We can't stay here," Veyra said eventually. "The Watchpost is too damaged, and we've outgrown what it can offer."

Grimjaw pulled out architectural plans Master Elena had given him. "The guild masters are talking about something that could house not just us, but visiting families, traveling craftsmen, regional gatherings. A true community center."

"So we do it?" Grimjaw asked. "Take the leap, build something permanent?"

"We do it," Veyra said. "For everyone who came here today because they consider us family."

Commander Atlock approached. "I'm recommending formal recognition as a Regional Civic Asset. Funding, legal protections, authority to operate across jurisdictions."

Veyra met his eyes. "On one condition. This doesn't change who we are. We remain people who help other people, who leave no one behind. If official recognition ever gets in the way of that, we walk away."

Atlock smiled. "I wouldn't expect anything else."

As families prepared to depart, each farewell carried warmth. Mrs. Blackwood pressed extra biscuits into Finn's hands. Little Sara from the trafficking rescue ran to Veyra, hugging her fiercely.

"I'll tell everyone about the brave lady with the sword who saves people," she promised solemnly.

Throughout the courtyard, personal connections were maintained—promises of future contact, the easy familiarity of people who knew they belonged to each other despite distance.

"Tomorrow," Veyra said as they prepared to seek their own rest, "we start planning for what comes next."

A sudden, joyful caw cut through the evening air—Echo's voice, bright with excitement. Aldwin was on his feet immediately.

Through the tent flap, Vera's eyes were open. When she saw Aldwin approaching, she managed the faintest smile.

"She's awake," Aldwin called back, his voice thick with relief.

Surrounded by the gentle sounds of their community settling in for the night, the Last Light Company allowed themselves to feel something they'd rarely had time for: simple, uncomplicated happiness.

Tomorrow would bring new challenges. But tonight, they were home, surrounded by love, and that was enough.
